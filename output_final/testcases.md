Such is the triumph, and the mystery, of this impossible enterprise that its slow (“journey-by-

journey, ” so to speak) evolution yields not so much a drama as a vast and moving meditation.

*Mirabile dictu: the textbook is a poem!*

‘The long genesis of Bach’s The Art of Fugue serves as one final example of the composer's

lifelong creative push toward the emendation, improvement, enlargement, or arrangement

*of already “finished” pieces. Having begun the work by 1742, Bach completed, within a year*

or two, a version containing twelve fugues and two canons. But in later years, moved by a

changing vision of the work, he continued to build, adding two canons, two fugues, and

*many other changes, down to new ornaments and various marks of articulation. The work’s*

*posthumous publication in 1751—supervised by C. P. E. Bach, among others—incorporated*

*this additional material (but also included material now viewed as extraneous to the work). In*

*this edition, each fugue is designated “contrapunctus. ” The final ordering of canons has been*

argued, but there is general agreement that the core sequence of Fugues 1-11 (or even 1-13)

clearly represents Bach’s own intention.

‘The variation principle at work in The Art of Fugue operates at several simultaneous levels. The

*most basic is thematic. Every fugue and canon contains material deriving from the work’s*

*main theme, the four-bar subject of Fugue 1. This linkage is at first direct, and—as the theme*

undergoes rhythmic and melodic alterations—becomes gradually less so as the work unfolds.

‘The theme itself is spare and simple, and possesses an expressive profile. Rather solemn, it sets

*an introspective and dignified tone for the entire work. It is the perfect foundation.*

A second, specifically structural level of variation is that of fugal type or procedure. The work’s

fourteen fugues are clearly grouped in an order of increasing operational complexity. We can

almost look upon each group as a separate chapter in a grand fugal adventure—but the blend

Fugues 1-4 are “simple” fugues—so called only in that special contrapuntal complica-

*tions have been omitted. These fugues, especially 1—3, are written in a style deriving from*

sixteenth-century vocal polyphony—a style naturally consonant with the theme—which fully

*establishes the work’s predominant and distinctive sound: austere, lucid, removed, beautiful.*

Thematic variation in these fugues is easily audible, Nos. 3 and 4 having inverted subjects.

Fugues 5—7 are “stretto” fugues, in which the subjects’ entries are made to overlap. All three

fugues use both the normal and inverted subject, which in all cases has now been embel-

lished with passing tones between the thirds. Fugue 6—in French Overture style, featuring

*dotted rhythms—displays diminution (i. e., contraction) of the theme. Fugue 7 contains both*

diminution and augmentation, in addition to the normal size.

Up to this point, only “primary” material (i. e., the main theme and variants of it) has been

used for fugal subjects. Now, by design, new themes are introduced, to be integrated with

*primary material, in a series of multiple-theme fugues, Nos. 8-11. Nos. 9 and 10 are highly*

contrasting double fugues (i. e., with two separately developed subjects). Nos. 8 and 11 are

triple fugues whose three subjects are thematically interrelated, though the fugues themselves,

both tremendously imposing, are utterly different one from the other. Fugue 11 represents for

me (and, I think, many others) the highest plateau of dramatic, architectural, and emotional

development to this point in the work. But no less impressive has been the long buildup,

powerfully sustained since the opening theme. Any serious, open-minded listener—including

those with no specialized training or technical understanding of the processes at hand—can

certainly apprehend by now (and probably long before now) that a very large, mysterious,

orderly, compelling, and ever-expanding world of polyphony has emanated from a small,

simple theme, whose presence, or essence, continues to be felt.

To continue, Fugues 12 and 13 are “mirror” fugues with contrasting material. Each employs

**a variant of the theme. And each comprises a pair of fugues: one “upright, ” the other with all**

voices strictly inverted.

*Movements 14-17 are (somewhat surprisingly) four canons of different types and themes*

(again, all of them variants of Theme 1). All are in two-part texture (itself a shock after fifty-

plus minutes in four-part texture) and brilliantly idiomatic for keyboard.

‘The final movement, a huge unfinished fugue, has engendered enormous comment and spec-

*ulation. Having the appearance of a triple fugue, it presents three themes in three sections,*

the last theme (B flat, A, C, B natural) spelling the name “Bach” in German nomenclature.

‘These letters (notes) have in fact been heard earlier, though less clearly, in the third theme

of Fugue 11, but there can be no doubt of the significance of this signature, in this piece, in

*this moment, in both the life of this piece and the life of the composer! Momentous as this*

entrance is, its exposition has been barely completed when, stunningly, the fugue “goes silent. ”

*And somehow, the shock of this sudden silence remains undiminished at every new hearing.*

‘There is no remedy for this. But it has long been surmised that Bach’s pen was not suspended

in mid-phrase, so to speak, by death. The consensus explanation is as follows: Bach’s true goal

*was for this movement to be a (final) quadruple fugue whose fourth theme would be the*

work’s opening subject, which, as early as the nineteenth century, was recognized to readily

combine with all three other subjects. A resolution of this sort, with a poignant re-entry of

the home theme and a unique inscription of his work by the composer—combined with

the unprecedented length of this fugue—makes a mighty argument for the movement as a

*conclusive one. It is also generally agreed that the final fugal integration would, by necessity,*

*have had to be fully worked out in advance of all the separate presentations of themes. Thus*

it becomes quite possible to imagine that the concluding section of both this fugue and of

the entire work actually did exist—at least in sketch form. It is no surprise, given the fugue’s

singular historical importance, its rich musical substance, and its advanced state of pregnancy,

so to speak, that, for more than a century, ambitious performer/composers (from Busoni to

‘Tovey to the present) have undertaken to “deliver” it. Because I have neither the skills nor the

*disposition to attempt such a completion, I have here opted (as many others have) to preserve*

Bach’s poignant silence.

#### ‘The Art of Fugue was first published in open-score notation, showing each voice individu-

ally, the performance instrument (or instruments) not specified. Thus the initial emphasis,

*which carried sway for many decades, seemed heavily weighted toward the work’s theoreti-*

*cal, abstract, even philosophical aspect, and especially its didactic intention—in spite of a*

long-existing tradition of similar open-scoring for erudite, polyphonic keyboard works by

*such composers as Frescobaldi, Scheidt, Froberger, and others (who must have served to some*

extent as models for Bach). And though many other indicators tend to confirm this work’s

keyboard identity, there can be no question that its demands can be impressively served by

other instrumental arrangements. In fact, the public performance history of The Art of Fugue

can be said to have begun in the wake of Wolfgang Graeser’s groundbreaking orchestration

of the work in 1927. Other instrumental transcriptions and organ arrangements followed.

*Harpsichord performances were slow to take hold. Landowska, Kirkpatrick, and other Bach*

specialists never recorded the work. But in recent decades recorded performances on harpsi-

chord, piano, and string quartet have become more numerous.

If the Goldberg Variations, with its overview of Baroque styles, is Bach’s “most worldly”

keyboard creation (as Charles Rosen has called it), The Art of Fugue, conceived at almost the

same time, would seem to be his most unworldly. Or otherworldly. My own regard for this

astonishing, strange, and soulful family of fugues and canons has been intimated earlier in

these notes. It is doubtful to me that Bach constructed, or intended, The Art of Fugue for full

performance. But he unquestionably regarded it as a summa of his art and had the highest

*hopes for its dispersion into the minds and ears of generations of musicians to come. Let us*

*not forget that throughout his lifetime of composing for keyboard, Back’s guiding precept*

was always to combine instruction with delight. I have been moved and nourished in my

*study of these pieces; therefore I have sought to lift them off the page—and to place them*

lovingly in the ears of those who would listen.

—Andrew Rangell
