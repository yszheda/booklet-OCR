THE ART OF FUGUE is Bach's final major instrumental composition—and a farewell testament for the ages. It is an ordered set of fourteen fugues and four canons, all deriving from a single theme, and all sharing the same key of D minor. A unique and prodigious demonstration of contrapuntal craft and imagination, the work was not fully completed at the time of Bach's death. It had been begun some eight years earlier, around 1741–42, as two other keyboard masterworks were being brought to fruition: the second book of *The Well-Tempered Clavier*, and the *Goldberg Variations*, both destined to reap a worldly popularity never to be accorded their more forbidding, inward-looking successor. In the last decade of his life, Bach withdrew from many of his long-standing activities as a composing cantor. And now, "alone with his genius," Bach joyfully and purposefully immersed himself in one self-assigned project after another, the culmination of a life's work, intending to place his own indelible stamp on the already vanishing art of pure instrumental counterpoint.

To see *The Art of Fugue* clearly for what it is, a description of the other above-mentioned monuments will help us. The WTC Book 2—like Book 1 of twenty years earlier—offers an enormous compendium of fugal types, themes, and treatments. It is a wondrous catalogue—but not a cyclical or unified single work. The *Goldberg Variations* is such a work, a perfect specimen of organic unity, filled to bursting with its organized family of canons, etudes, and character-pieces—but designed for optimal effect in a single listening. *The Art of Fugue* is neither of these, but something of a hybrid: its many fugues and canons—which form a comprehensive illustrated manual of counterpoint—are all linked to the simple subject of Fugue 1. Thus they are a "set of variations." Not, however, in the usual sense. In a conventional variations-set, individual variations must make their point efficiently and then exit the stage, for the sake of continuity. Contrasts of character and tempo are achieved through changes in texture. In *The Art of Fugue*, practically every movement is long, elaborate, even arduous. Texture is largely unchanging. The D-minor tonality is a constant. The language is austere, the tone serious—even through contrast. The "variations" unfold at a glacial rate. And yet!

Such is the triumph, and the mystery, of this impossible enterprise that its slow ("journey-by-journey," so to speak) evolution yields not so much a drama as a vast and moving meditation. *Mirabile dictu*: the textbook is a poem!

The long genesis of Bach's *The Art of Fugue* serves as one final example of the composer's lifelong creative push toward the emendation, improvement, enlargement, or arrangement of already "finished" pieces. Having begun the work by 1742, Bach completed, within a year or two, a version containing twelve fugues and two canons. But in later years, moved by a changing vision of the work, he continued to build, adding two canons, two fugues, and many other changes, down to new ornaments and various marks of articulation. The work's posthumous publication in 1751—supervised by C.P.E. Bach, among others—incorporated this additional material (but also included material now viewed as extraneous to the work). In this edition, each fugue is designated "contrapunctus." The final ordering of canons has been argued, but there is general agreement that the core sequence of Fugues 1–11 (or even 1–13) clearly represents Bach's own intention.

The variation principle at work in *The Art of Fugue* operates at several simultaneous levels. The most basic is thematic. Every fugue and canon contains material deriving from the work's main theme, the four-bar subject of Fugue 1. This linkage is at first direct, and—as the theme undergoes rhythmic and melodic alterations—becomes gradually less so as the work unfolds. The theme itself is spare and simple, and possesses an expressive profile. Rather solemn, it sets an introspective and dignified tone for the entire work. It is the perfect foundation.

A second, specifically structural level of variation is that of fugal type or procedure. The work's fourteen fugues are clearly grouped in an order of increasing operational complexity. We can almost look upon each group as a separate chapter in a grand fugal adventure—but the blend is achieved seamlessly. Here is an overview:

Fugues 1–4 are "simple" fugues—so called only in that special contrapuntal complications have been omitted. These fugues, especially 1–3, are written in a style deriving from sixteenth-century vocal polyphony—a style naturally consonant with the theme—which fully establishes the work's predominant and distinctive sound: austere, lucid, removed, beautiful. Thematic variation in these fugues is easily audible, Nos. 3 and 4 having inverted subjects.

Fugues 5–7 are "stretto" fugues, in which the subjects' entries are made to overlap. All three fugues use both the normal and inverted subject, which in all cases has now been embellished with passing tones between the thirds. Fugue 6—in French Overture style, featuring dotted rhythms—displays diminution (i.e., contraction) of the theme. Fugue 7 contains both diminution and augmentation, in addition to the normal size.

Up to this point, only "primary" material (i.e., the main theme and variants of it) has been used for fugal subjects. Now, by design, new themes are introduced, to be integrated with primary material, in a series of multiple-theme fugues, Nos. 8–11. Nos. 9 and 10 are highly contrasting double fugues (i.e., with two separately developed subjects). Nos. 8 and 11 are triple fugues whose three subjects are thematically interrelated, though the fugues themselves, both tremendously imposing, are utterly different one from the other. Fugue 11 represents for me (and, I think, many others) the highest plateau of dramatic, architectural, and emotional development to this point in the work. But no less impressive has been the long buildup, powerfully sustained since the opening theme. Any serious, open-minded listener—including those with no specialized training or technical understanding of the processes at hand—can certainly apprehend by now (and probably long before now) that a very large, mysterious, orderly, compelling, and ever-expanding world of polyphony has emanated from a small, simple theme, whose presence, or essence, continues to be felt.

To continue, Fugues 12 and 13 are "mirror" fugues with contrasting material. Each employs a variant of the theme. And each comprises a pair of fugues: one "upright," the other with all voices strictly inverted.

Movements 14–17 are (somewhat surprisingly) four canons of different types and themes (again, all of them variants of Theme 1). All are in two-part texture (itself a shock after fifty-plus minutes in four-part texture) and brilliantly idiomatic for keyboard.

The final movement, a huge unfinished fugue, has engendered enormous comment and speculation. Having the appearance of a triple fugue, it presents three themes in three sections, the last theme (B flat, A, C, B natural) spelling the name "Bach" in German nomenclature. These letters (notes) have in fact been heard earlier, though less clearly, in the third theme of Fugue 11, but there can be no doubt of the significance of this signature, in this piece, in this moment, in both the life of this piece and the life of the composer! Momentous as this entrance is, its exposition has been barely completed when, stunningly, the fugue "goes silent." And somehow, the shock of this sudden silence remains undiminished at every new hearing. There is no remedy for this. But it has long been surmised that Bach's pen was not suspended in mid-phrase, so to speak, by death. The consensus explanation is as follows: Bach's true goal was for this movement to be a (final) quadruple fugue whose fourth theme would be the work's opening subject, which, as early as the nineteenth century, was recognized to readily combine with all three other subjects. A resolution of this sort, with a poignant re-entry of the home theme and a unique inscription of his work by the composer—combined with the unprecedented length of this fugue—makes a mighty argument for the movement as a conclusive one. It is also generally agreed that the final fugal integration would, by necessity, have had to be fully worked out in advance of all the separate presentations of themes. Thus it becomes quite possible to imagine that the concluding section of both this fugue and of the entire work actually did exist—at least in sketch form. It is no surprise, given the fugue's singular historical importance, its rich musical substance, and its advanced state of pregnancy, so to speak, that, for more than a century, ambitious performer/composers (from Busoni to Tovey to the present) have undertaken to "deliver" it. Because I have neither the skills nor the disposition to attempt such a completion, I have here opted (as many others have) to preserve Bach's poignant silence.

*The Art of Fugue* was first published in open-score notation, showing each voice individually, and the performance instrument (or instruments) not specified. Thus the initial emphasis, which carried sway for many decades, seemed heavily weighted toward the work's theoretical, abstract, even philosophical aspect, and especially its didactic intention—in spite of a long-existing tradition of similar open-scoring for erudite, polyphonic keyboard works by such composers as Frescobaldi, Scheidt, Froberger, and others (who must have served to some extent as models for Bach). And though many other indicators tend to confirm this work's keyboard identity, there can be no question that its demands can be impressively served by other instrumental arrangements. In fact, the public performance history of *The Art of Fugue* can be said to have begun in the wake of Wolfgang Graeser's groundbreaking orchestration of the work in 1927. Other instrumental transcriptions and organ arrangements followed. Harpsichord performances were slow to take hold. Landowska, Kirkpatrick, and other Bach specialists never recorded the work. But in recent decades recorded performances on harpsichord, piano, and string quartet have become more numerous.

If the *Goldberg Variations*, with its overview of Baroque styles, is Bach's "most worldly" keyboard creation (as Charles Rosen has called it), *The Art of Fugue*, conceived at almost the same time, would seem to be his most unworldly. Or otherworldly. My own regard for this astonishing, strange, and soulful family of fugues and canons has been intimated earlier in these notes. It is doubtful to me that Bach constructed, or intended, *The Art of Fugue* for full performance. But he unquestionably regarded it as a summa of his art and had the highest hopes for its dispersion into the minds and ears of generations of musicians to come. Let us not forget that throughout his lifetime of composing for keyboard, Bach's guiding precept was always to combine instruction with delight. I have been moved and nourished in my study of these pieces; therefore I have sought to lift them off the page—and to place them lovingly in the ears of those who would listen.

—Andrew Rangell

PIANIST ANDREW RANGELL has recorded 25 discs on the Dorian, Bridge, and Steinway & Sons labels. Some two decades ago Mr. Rangell's recording debut featured highly acclaimed performances of Bach's *Goldberg Variations*, F-sharp minor Toccata, and the two ricercares from *The Musical Offering*. More recently, superlative recordings of the Partitas, the French Suites, *The Well-Tempered Clavier* Book 1, the *Italian Concerto*, the B-minor French Overture, and other works have established Mr. Rangell as among the truly distinguished Bach interpreters of the present day. His many other recordings reveal Mr. Rangell to be an artist of exceptional scope and affinity. These discs range from composers such as Sweelinck, Gibbons, Tisdale, and Froberger through several centuries to Stravinsky, Enescu, Schoenberg, Ives, Nielsen, Fartein Valen, and Christian Wolff. Recently Mr. Rangell has created a new disc for future release, featuring folk-influenced masterworks of Bartók, Kodály, and Janáček.

Mr. Rangell made his New York debut as winner of the Malraux Award of the Concert Artists' Guild and has since performed and lectured throughout the United States and in Europe and Israel. He has also taught on the faculties of Dartmouth College, Middlebury College, and Tufts University. In the 1980s, already recognized as a distinctive recitalist and collaborative artist, Mr. Rangell gained national attention—and the award of an Avery Fisher Career Grant—for his vivid and probing traversal of the complete Beethoven sonata cycle on stage in New York, Boston, Cleveland, Rochester, Denver, and other U.S. cities. A hand injury sustained in 1991 forced Mr. Rangell to gradually alter the trajectory of his career, and eventually to place his highest priority on recording. In recent years he has created several DVDs for children—integrating his special talents as author, illustrator, narrator, and pianist.
